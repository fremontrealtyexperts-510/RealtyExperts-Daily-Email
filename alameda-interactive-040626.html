Inventory Falls for Fifth Straight Week — But the Pipeline Is Building

Total inventory across our 16-city tracker fell another 21 units to 2,501 — the fifth straight week of decline. But new listings rebounded to 176 (from 156 last week), and coming-soon jumped to 261 (from 250). The pipeline is filling, even as total supply keeps tightening. Strong jobs numbers and surging oil are keeping the Fed on hold — and mortgage rates stuck near 6.5% heading into peak spring buying season.

Union City — Down 7 to Just 82 Total

Biggest percentage drop this week. Union City shed 7 listings to just 82 total, making it one of the tightest markets in the tracker — and getting tighter. Only 7 new listings and 6 coming soon. Buyers have very limited options here.

Fremont — 296 Total (Up 2)

Fremont ticked up 2 to 296 total listings. New listings jumped to 27 (from 19 last week), while coming-soon pulled back to 54 (from 59). Sellers are starting their spring move. Townhome active sits at 44 with 12 pending.

Milpitas — Only 1 New Listing All Week

Up 3 to 116 total, but here is the stat of the week — only 1 new listing across the entire city. Coming-soon dropped to 30 (from 33). Townhome pending (34) still exceeds active (23) by 11 units. Inventory is building from pending conversions, not new sellers.

Oakland & Newark

Oakland dropped 4 to 732 with 42 new listings — buyers continue to absorb faster than sellers can list. Newark held flat at 101 for the second week near the century mark, with 15 coming soon and 8 new this week.

Private Credit Cracks Widen

Private credit loan defaults jumped to 9.2% — more than triple the historical norm of 2-2.5%. AI startups are eating into the software companies these funds bet heavily on. Meanwhile, big investment firms are pivoting to warehouses and apartments for stable income and cutting office and retail bets, where deal volumes fell 46% last quarter. If more businesses default, the ripple could hit jobs, pensions, and retirement funds.

Airlines Feeling the Heat; Jobs Surprise to the Upside

Airlines are feeling the heat from surging fuel costs. United said it burned through $400 million more than expected last month and is raising checked bag fees by $10 to cushion the blow. Delta reports earnings this week — expect turbulence in its outlook too.

On the trade front, Trump rolled out pharma tariffs: zero for foreign drugmakers who manufacture in the U.S., 100% for everyone else — with a 120-day window to comply. Wall Street expected worse. Novo Nordisk, GSK, and AstraZeneca all popped over 1%.

Bright spot: March jobs crushed expectations — 178,000 added vs. the 59,000 estimate, and unemployment ticked down to 4.3%. But wage growth slipped to 3.5%, the lowest since 2021. Employers are hiring — just not paying more for it.

Oil Remains the Story; Strait of Hormuz Still Choked

Brent crude hit $141 a barrel last week — its highest since 2008 — as the Strait of Hormuz blockade continues to choke 20% of global supply. Ship traffic through the strait has fallen from 150 per day to under 20. Gas is now $4.08 nationally, up $1.10 since the war started. Bay Area drivers are still paying well north of $6.

The strong jobs report adds a wrinkle: great news for employment, but it gives the Fed less reason to cut rates anytime soon. Gold pulled back slightly to $4,672 as investors weigh war uncertainty against economic resilience. The 10-year yield dipped to 4.31% — bond markets pricing in slower wage growth despite the hiring beat.

Schwab Opens Crypto to 46 Million Clients

Crypto ticked higher — BTC rose to $67,505 and ETH held near $2,065, while XRP slipped to $1.30. The bigger story is institutional adoption. Charles Schwab is launching direct Bitcoin and Ether trading for its 46 million clients in H1 2026. Morgan Stanley is prepping crypto through E*Trade. When firms managing $12 trillion start putting crypto on the same menu as stocks and bonds, it is no longer the fringe — it is the appetizer.

Coinbase also got conditional approval for digital asset custody, joining Ripple and Circle. The exchange said it will not offer banking services like deposits or lending — for now. Baby steps toward legitimacy.
