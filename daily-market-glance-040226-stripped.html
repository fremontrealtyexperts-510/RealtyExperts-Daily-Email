Mortgage rates ticked up to 6.47% for the 30-year fixed — a reversal from earlier this week — while home flipping profits nationally hit their lowest level since 2008.
If your house was shaking at 1:41 AM this morning, you weren't dreaming. A 4.6-magnitude earthquake struck near Boulder Creek in the Santa Cruz Mountains — about 30 miles from Fremont. Originally reported as a 4.9, the USGS downgraded it within hours. Residents in Fremont, San Ramon, Milpitas, and Los Gatos reported moderate to strong shaking. No major damage or injuries, but the USGS gives a 60% chance of a 3.0+ aftershock this week. Homeowners and buyers: this is your reminder to check your earthquake insurance — standard California policies do NOT include it.
Total inventory across our 16-city tracker slipped another 21 units to 2,526 — the third straight week of decline. But coming-soon listings jumped to 249 (up 27 from last week), so a spring pipeline is forming. New listings dipped slightly to 203.
📍 Milpitas: Still the hottest segment in the Tri-City area. Townhome pending (34) now exceeds active (26) by 8 — the widest gap we've tracked. Coming-soon surged to 33 (up from 28 last week), so a wave may be heading to market. For now, buyers are absorbing homes faster than they hit MLS. Total inventory dropped 5 to 118.
📍 Fremont: Inventory continues to tighten — down 5 to 297 total, despite 58 coming-soon listings (up from 50 last week). Condo actives rose from 59 to 66, but detached home actives fell from 95 to 87. Only 18 new listings hit this week (down from 27). The pipeline is building but not converting yet.
📍 Union City: Up 4 to 88 total with 8 new listings — double last week. Coming-soon ticked up to 7. Still the tightest market in the area, but showing signs of life.
📍 Newark: Added 7 units to reach 99 — the biggest percentage jump among Tri-Cities this week. Only 5 new listings, so the increase came from coming-soon conversions and slower absorption.
📍 Dublin: The week's biggest mover — up 8 to 151, with 14 new listings and 26 coming-soon. Dublin is attracting more inventory than almost any other city in the tracker right now.
On the national front, home flipping profits have hit their lowest level since 2008 — averaging just $66,000 per flip. High prices, rising rates, and softer buyer interest are squeezing margins before flippers reach the closing table.
Hedge fund manager Bill Ackman is pushing for Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae to part ways with the federal government. Both mortgage giants have been in conservatorship since 2008 and together guarantee millions of U.S. mortgages. If privatization happens, it could reshape how Americans access home loans — for better or worse.

SpaceX quietly filed its S-1 with the SEC yesterday — and if the numbers hold, it could be the biggest IPO in history. On the health front, Eli Lilly earned an FDA nod for its daily weight-loss pill, pushing the stock up 3.74%. Competitor Novo Nordisk (Ozempic) didn't love the news — dropping over 1.5% at one point. Lilly is now seeking approval in 40+ countries.

The U.S. added 62,000 private-sector jobs in March — beating the 39,000 Wall Street expected. But zoom in and the picture gets lopsided: education, health, and construction created 88,000 positions by themselves. Every other sector? Net negative. Small businesses (under 50 employees) carried the load with 85,000 new hires, while mid-size and large firms actually cut staff. The job market is running on two engines while the rest are in the shop.
Gold had its best day in about two weeks, climbing 2.38% to $4,758 after reports that Iran ceasefire talks are happening behind closed doors. President Trump addressed the nation last night and said the war could end in 2-3 weeks. If peace brings lower oil and cooler inflation, gold stands to benefit.
Oil is another story — WTI crude dropped 5.76% to $111.53 as ceasefire optimism deflated the war premium. When war premiums unwind, they unwind fast. The International Energy Agency is watching closely and considering releasing strategic reserves if prices stay volatile. Bay Area drivers are still paying over $6 a gallon, but if ceasefire talks hold, relief could come by late April.
