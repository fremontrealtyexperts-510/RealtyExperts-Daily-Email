For the first time since the Iran war kicked off in late February, the 30-year fixed dipped slightly. The ceasefire news pushed 10-year Treasury yields below 4.3%, and mortgage rates followed. But the celebration could be short-lived — yields and oil are already ticking back up on doubts about whether the ceasefire holds.
Mortgage applications fell for the third straight week, even with rates drifting down from the 6.47% March high. Some analysts think if the ceasefire sticks, rates could test 6% by June. That's a big "if," but it's the first real glimmer of relief buyers have had in months.
Nationally, the high-end is cracking. The top 10% of U.S. home prices are down 2.9% year-over-year, even though 13 metro areas still have median prices above $1 million (five of them in California). Affordability is squeezing buyers at every tier.
Some cities are going to extremes to attract new residents. Detroit is offering up to $15,000 to people who buy a home or bring a business to the Motor City. Baltimore's "Live Near Your Work" program pays $2,000–$5,000. Nothing like that happening in the Bay anytime soon — but worth keeping on the radar.
💵 Minimum Wage Increase: The City of Fremont has announced that the local minimum wage will officially increase to $18.05 per hour starting July 1, 2026.
🤖 Autonomous Delivery Robots: The city recently kicked off pilot programs like "Dine Local with Dot," featuring DoorDash's autonomous delivery robots navigating local sidewalks to deliver restaurant orders.
🏛️ Central Park Community Center: The city recently held a groundbreaking ceremony for the highly anticipated new Central Park Community Center, a major investment in local recreational infrastructure.

Inflation is stuck in neutral. Core CPI (excluding food and energy) ticked slightly below January's number, still hovering around 3% year-over-year. The catch: that data reflects conditions before the Iran war fully gripped the economy — so next month's read could look very different.
Growth just got a haircut. Q4 2025 GDP was initially pegged at 1.4% — the revised figure just landed at 0.5%. Last fall's government shutdown dragged activity, and consumer and business spending slowed to 1.9% quarter over quarter, the softest pace in a while.
Gold took a breather at $4,775 after its recent climb — the ceasefire softened safe-haven demand, at least for a day. WTI Crude is still hovering near $98, with every headline out of the Middle East moving the needle. If the ceasefire cracks, both could swing fast in opposite directions.

Bitcoin is back in rally mode, and the shorts are paying for it. Roughly $600 million in crypto positions got liquidated over just a few days after the ceasefire news hit. Traders who bet Bitcoin would keep falling lost about $398 million — the most aggressive short squeeze in over a month.
In other crypto news — the mystery of Bitcoin's creator may (finally) have an answer. The New York Times is pointing to Blockstream CEO Adam Back as Satoshi Nakamoto, the anonymous inventor of Bitcoin. Back denies it, and Blockstream called the claim "speculation, not definitive cryptographic proof." If he ever admits it, he'd instantly become one of the wealthiest people alive — Satoshi's wallet holds roughly 1 million BTC, worth over $72 billion at current prices.
