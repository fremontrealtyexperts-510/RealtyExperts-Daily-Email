Spring inventory is here, but it's shrinking fast — every Tri-City market lost listings this week as buyer absorption outpaced new supply.
Across the 16 cities we track, total inventory dropped from 2,600 to 2,567 — a net loss of 33 listings in just days. Buyers are moving faster than sellers can list.
📍 Milpitas: The hottest market in the Tri-City area right now. Townhomes are flying: 33 pending vs. just 27 active, with only 2 new TH listings. If you're a buyer looking at Milpitas townhomes, be ready to compete. The city is also booming with $450M+ in new development investment — a 5-year high — including 1M+ sq ft of manufacturing and R&D facilities, the new 102-unit Sango Court affordable housing near BART, and 206 townhomes approved at California Circle (75 units reserved for teachers).
📍 Fremont: Still the largest inventory pool at 304 listings, but down 5 from last report. Detached homes dominate (90 active, 67 pending). The 53 coming-soon listings suggest a pipeline, but only 26 new listings actually hit the market. Big local news: H Mart is bringing its largest U.S. store to Pacific Commons — a 100,000 sq ft two-story flagship with a food hall, restaurants, and entertainment, replacing the old Kohl's. Construction starts late 2026. Also, a $10M SuperLotto ticket was sold at the 7-Eleven on Central Ave last Saturday.
📍 Union City: The tightest market — only 86 total listings and just 1 new listing this period. Buyers here have almost no options. The city is updating its Parks & Recreation Master Plan for the first time in 25 years.
📍 Hayward & Newark: Hayward leads in new listings (21) but still lost 4 total as absorption outpaced supply. Newark holds steady at 98 with 6 new. Both remain competitive seller's markets.
On the macro side, Trump's tariffs are adding $9,200-$25,000 to new home costs (lumber, steel, copper hit hardest), and the NAHB estimates 450,000 fewer homes will be built through 2030. Japanese construction firms have quietly scooped up 23 American homebuilders since 2020, leveraging near-zero interest rates at home to outbid domestic competitors. Meanwhile, Cisco is cutting 221 jobs at its Milpitas offices as Bay Area tech companies restructure around AI.

Oil is the story right now. Brent crude has surged 50% in the past month as the Strait of Hormuz — which moves 20% of the world's oil — remains blocked due to the Iran conflict. Experts warn that if it doesn't reopen within two weeks, prices could stay elevated long-term.
The ripple effect is already hitting Bay Area wallets: gas prices have topped $6 a gallon across the region, with some analysts warning San Francisco could see $10. Two California refinery shutdowns (Phillips 66 Wilmington, Valero Benicia) wiped out 17% of in-state refining capacity, making things even worse.
Despite the chaos, Fed Chair Powell says don't expect a rate hike from this oil shock — his argument is that by the time any hike would kick in, the energy crunch could be over. Markets aren't so sure: they're now pricing in a greater chance of a hike than a cut this year.
