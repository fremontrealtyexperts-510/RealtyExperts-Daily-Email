Mortgage rates slid to 6.29% — down for the second straight day — but applications fell 10.5% last week as the spring market stays frozen by energy costs and economic uncertainty.
Total inventory across our 16-city tracker dropped another 41 units to 2,526 — the second straight week of decline. Buyers are absorbing homes faster than sellers can list them, and the spring thaw hasn't materialized the way it usually does.
📍 Milpitas: Still the hottest segment in the Tri-City area. Townhome pending (34) now exceeds active (26) by an even wider margin than last week. Only 2 new TH listings hit the market — again. If you're a buyer eyeing Milpitas townhomes, expect multiple offers. The city is also asking residents to weigh in on its 2026 Community Survey at milpitas.gov/communityinput to guide budget and service priorities.
📍 Fremont: Inventory continues to tighten — down 7 to 297 total, despite 58 coming-soon listings in the pipeline. Detached homes remain the core of the market with 87 active and 69 pending. Only 18 new listings actually hit this period. On the development front, H Mart's 100,000 sq ft flagship at Pacific Commons is still on track for late 2026 construction.
📍 Union City: Finally got some breathing room — up 2 to 88 total with 4 new listings (vs. just 1 last week). Still the tightest market in the area, but heading in the right direction. The city's BART Mural Project is bringing community-designed art to 9 BART pillars in the Decoto neighborhood alongside Kennedy Park.
📍 Hayward & Newark: Hayward barely moved (-1 to 213) with 18 new listings keeping pace with demand. South Hayward BART station is temporarily closed after a fire damaged equipment. Hayward Restaurant Month is underway — great time to support local businesses. Newark ticked up 1 to 99, and is hosting a Spring Career Fair today at 39399 Cherry St.
On the national front, homeowners are sitting on record levels of equity — but 97% of it went untapped in 2025. Why? They're locked into pandemic-era 3% rates and have zero incentive to refinance. That means fewer move-up buyers entering the market, which keeps inventory tight at the entry level.
The big wildcard: if Iran ceasefire talks succeed, gas prices could drop from $6 to $3.50 by May, easing the economic squeeze that's kept buyers on the sidelines.
