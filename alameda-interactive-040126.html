Inventory Drops Again — Second Straight Weekly Decline

Total inventory across 16 Tri-City and Alameda County markets fell another 41 units to 2,526 — the second consecutive week of decline. Spring is supposed to bring more listings, not fewer. Mortgage applications dropped 10.5% last week, and refinance activity plunged 15%. Homeowners are sitting on record equity — 97% untapped — but most are locked into pandemic-era 3% rates and have zero incentive to move.

Milpitas — Townhome Market Running Away

Milpitas townhomes remain the hottest segment in the region: 34 pending versus just 26 active, with only 2 new TH listings this week. The gap between buyers and available supply actually widened. If you are a TH owner in Milpitas thinking about selling, this is as favorable a market as you will find. The city is also rolling out a free doorbell camera program for residents.

Fremont — 297 Total Listings (Down 7)

Fremont inventory dropped to 297 total listings with 58 coming soon. The tightening continues into a third week. On the retail front, the H Mart flagship at Pacific Commons — 100,000 sq ft, the largest in the Bay Area — remains on track for a late 2026 opening.

Union City — Finally Some Relief

Union City added 2 listings to reach 88, with 4 new listings this week — a welcome uptick for buyers who have been starved for options. The Decoto district is getting a cultural boost with the BART Mural Project, adding public art near the station.

Hayward & Newark

Hayward edged down to 213 listings (minus 1), with 18 new this week. South Hayward BART station is temporarily closed after a fire — check alternate routes. Restaurant Month is underway with deals across the city. Newark ticked up to 99 listings (plus 1), and the city is hosting a Spring Career Fair today.

The Iran Ceasefire Wild Card

Here is the number everyone should be watching: if the U.S. and Iran reach a ceasefire deal by April 6, analysts project Bay Area gas could drop from $6+ to roughly $3.50 by May. Oil surged 60% in March — the biggest monthly jump since 1988 — and that spike has rippled into everything from commute costs to construction materials. A deal would be the single biggest relief valve for household budgets since the pandemic stimulus checks.

Jobs Picture Weaker Than Reported

The Bureau of Labor Statistics quietly revised JOLTS data: 2025 actually added just 181,000 jobs, not 584,000 as originally reported. That is the weakest hiring year since the pandemic. Meanwhile, Oracle is cutting 30,000 jobs despite posting a $6 billion quarter. The disconnect between corporate profits and hiring should concern anyone counting on a strong job market to sustain home prices.

Massive Rally on Iran Ceasefire Hopes

Markets staged the biggest single-day rally of 2026: the S&P 500 surged 2.91%, the Dow gained 2.49%, and the Nasdaq led with a 3.83% pop. The catalyst was renewed optimism around an Iran ceasefire deal, which would ease the energy crisis that has weighed on markets all month. Whether this holds depends entirely on whether talks produce results by the April 6 deadline.

Oil Pushes Past $118; Gas Pain Continues

WTI crude rose another 1.32% to $118.35, extending a March that saw oil prices spike 60% — the largest monthly gain since 1988. Bay Area gas is well past $6 per gallon. Gold pulled back 1.09% but remains above $4,400 as safe-haven demand persists. The 10-year yield dropped to 4.29%, which has already begun pulling mortgage rates lower — the 30-year fixed fell 7 basis points to 6.29%, the second straight day of decline.

Bitcoin slipped 0.85% to $67,657 while Ethereum dropped 0.90% to $2,085 and XRP eased 0.52% to $1.33.
